The Opportunity — India Context

BAMBOO & BIOCHAR:
INDIA'S UNDERUTILISED ASSET

India holds the world's second-largest bamboo resource base — 13.96 million hectares — yet extracts a fraction of its economic value. Annual supply of ~14.6 MT against domestic demand of 26 MT creates a structural deficit that the National Bamboo Mission and state governments are trying to address through plantation expansion. The 2017 Forest Act amendment (de-classifying bamboo grown outside forests as a "tree") was the catalytic policy shift that opened private bamboo cultivation at scale.

The biochar opportunity is even more nascent but moving fast. Two of the world's largest carbon removal deals ever signed — Google and Microsoft's contracts with Indian startup Varaha — are India-based. Tata Steel became the first Indian steel manufacturer to use biochar in production (replacing 30,000 tonnes of fossil fuel) in November 2024. The global biochar carbon removal market grew 131.6% CAGR between 2022–2024. India, with its agricultural residue base and bamboo stock, is uniquely positioned.

Critical gap — Bombay Bioenergy The agarbatti opportunity is significantly underappreciated. India imports 5,000–6,000 t/month of D. strictus (male bamboo) sticks from China and Vietnam. A 25% import duty was reinstated in 2020. The domestic industry is ₹6,000 crore and growing — and it specifically needs D. strictus, which is the most abundant natural bamboo in Maharashtra (Gadchiroli, Chandrapur). A high-precision stick manufacturing line alongside bioenergy production could serve two completely different markets from the same feedstock, with agarbatti sticks commanding ₹8,000–18,000/MT vs ₹7,500–11,000/MT for SAMARTH pellets.

Bombay Bioenergy — Carbon Credit Opportunity Varaha's Google deal (100,000 tonnes CDR by 2030) and Microsoft deal (100,000+ tonnes) are the most important data points for Bamboo Bioenergy. Both are from Maharashtra and Gujarat — Bombay Bioenergy's home states. Both use agricultural residue (cotton waste). Bamboo biochar from Maharashtra would have the same or better carbon sequestration credentials. The pathway: produce biochar → certify with Puro.Earth (Varaha is the only listed India entity currently) → sell CDR credits at $200–600/tCO₂ while also selling the physical biochar to Tata Steel or agricultural buyers. This is a genuine two-revenue-stream play. Varaha is a potential partner, not just a reference point.
Tata Steel Precedent Tata Steel Jamshedpur replaced 30,000 tonnes of fossil fuel with biochar (November 2024) — the first such use in India. Potential: 50,000+ tonnes CO₂ reduction annually. India government has a steel-biochar task force active since March 2024. JSW, SAIL in exploratory discussions. At Bombay Bioenergy's scale, a dedicated high-fixed-carbon biochar line for steel PCI would find a buyer before construction is complete. Price: ₹12,000–20,000/MT — significantly above SAMARTH pellet pricing.

Wood Vinegar — Japan Export Opportunity Japan uses "mokusaku" (wood vinegar) extensively in organic agriculture and cosmetics. India currently has no significant commercial wood vinegar producer. The Japan market pays $300–2,000/MT. If Bombay Bioenergy's pyrolysis system collects wood vinegar separately — an equipment modification costing ₹5–15 lakh per reactor — it adds a high-value export product stream with zero additional feedstock cost. 150 kg/tonne bamboo × ₹40,000/MT = ₹6,000 additional revenue per tonne bamboo processed. Compounding on 10,000 MT/yr scale: ₹6 Cr additional annual revenue from material currently being wasted.
