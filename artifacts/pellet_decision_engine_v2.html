01 — Power · torque · RPM — the locked trinity
⊛
The equation that governs everything
Power (kW) = Torque (Nm) × RPM ÷ 9,550. This is exact — not an estimate. If a vendor gives you all three numbers and they don't satisfy this equation, one of them is wrong. Use this to verify every datasheet.
⟳
Pellet mills need torque, not speed
The die must rotate at 100–180 RPM to give biomass adequate dwell time in the hole for lignin activation. What pushes material through is force — torque. Two 132 kW machines with different gearbox ratios have completely different force at the die face and completely different real throughput.
↓
Speed reduction multiplies torque
A 10:1 gearbox reduces motor RPM by 10× and multiplies torque by 10× (minus friction losses). So a motor producing 869 Nm at 1450 RPM becomes 8,180 Nm at 145 RPM at the die shaft (at 94% gearbox efficiency). That 8,180 Nm is what makes pellets.
≋
Why fast die speed kills quality
Peripheral die speed should be 5–7 m/s for most agri-biomass. Above 7 m/s, biomass passes through the hole before lignin activates — pellets look fine hot but crumble on cooling. Budget machines run higher RPM to inflate TPH numbers on sawdust. On your biomass it means failed PDI.
Motor torque (Nm) = kW × 9,550 ÷ motor RPM
Die shaft torque (Nm) = Motor torque × gearbox ratio × transmission efficiency
Die RPM = Motor RPM ÷ gearbox ratio
Peripheral speed (m/s) = π × die diameter (m) × die RPM ÷ 60
Target: die shaft torque ≥ 8,000 Nm for 132 kW on agri-residue · peripheral speed 5–7 m/s · die RPM 100–180
02 — Transmission types compared
Belt drive (Poly-V / V-belt) — Andritz, CPM standard

Motor drives die shaft via belt and pulley. Speed reduction achieved by pulley diameter ratio, not gears. Efficiency: Poly-V 88–91%, V-belt 85–88%. Lower than gearbox — but this is intentional design philosophy.

The belt is a mechanical fuse. When a stone, wire or die block causes a force spike, the belt slips or snaps first. Replacement belt: ₹2,000–8,000, 30 minutes. Replacement gearbox: ₹3–15 lakh, several days. For Indian agri-residue with contaminated feedstock, this is a significant practical advantage.

Also provides slight elastic compliance — momentary load spikes from uneven fibrous feed are absorbed by belt stretch before reaching the motor. Fewer trips, smoother operation on difficult material.

Quality gearbox — SEW, Flender, Siemens

Helical or planetary gears in sealed oil bath. Efficiency 92–94%. Rigid torque delivery — what the ratio gives, it gives consistently. Higher efficiency means more of your motor kW reaches the die.

Failure mode is catastrophic — a gear tooth failure or bearing seizure destroys the gearbox housing. Replacement is expensive and has long lead times, especially for imported units in India.

Best suited when feedstock is very clean and consistent (e.g. dedicated sawdust plant) and when maximum kW-to-TPH efficiency matters.

Local cast gearbox (unknown brand) — the hidden risk

Many budget Chinese and Indian-branded machines use locally manufactured gearboxes with no published specifications. Actual efficiency 78–85%, often lower due to manufacturing tolerances. No published torque rating means you cannot verify the die shaft torque calculation. Bearing quality is highly variable. In practice, these fail at 3,000–6,000 operating hours on hard biomass. Ask for the gearbox manufacturer's nameplate. If the vendor cannot name it, walk away or price in a gearbox replacement at year 2–3.

Direct drive — rare, high-end only

Motor shaft IS the die shaft. No intermediate transmission — 97–99% efficiency. Requires a purpose-built low-RPM, high-torque motor (not a standard induction motor). These motors cost 3–5× a standard motor and have 6–12 month lead times for replacement in India. Only found on large-format European machines above 400 kW. Do not confuse with a vendor claiming "direct drive" on a gearbox machine — verify by asking for the motor nameplate RPM and confirming it matches die RPM.

The die cost trap

A cheap machine with basic X46Cr13 dies running on rice straw will replace dies every 800–1,000 hours. At 16 hrs/day that's every 50–62 days. At ₹1.5L per replacement, that's ₹9–12L/year in die costs alone — more than the annual maintenance budget of a European machine with D2 dies lasting 2,500+ hours. Calculate total cost of ownership, not just purchase price.

05 — Questions that cannot be faked
Technical demands

1. TPH tested on which material, moisture %, particle size?
2. Gearbox output shaft torque in Nm at rated motor RPM?
3. Die RPM at full rated load?
4. L/D ratio of dies supplied for MY feedstock?
5. PDI (Pellet Durability Index) at claimed TPH?
6. Motor amp draw log at full load — not nameplate amps.
7. Ring die claim: show cross-section engineering drawing of die assembly.
8. Gearbox: nameplate photo showing manufacturer and ratio.

Commercial demands (in contract)

1. Performance guarantee: minimum TPH on YOUR feedstock at YOUR moisture, with penalty clause.
2. Die + roller warranty: minimum 2,000 operating hours or 12 months.
3. Commissioning: named engineer on-site, minimum 7 days, vendor cost.
4. Spare parts: rollers + die shell available in India ≤72 hours.
5. Reference visit: operating plant in India on same feedstock before deposit.
6. Payment terms: max 30% advance, 60% FAT, 10% after 30 days commissioning.
